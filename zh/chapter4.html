How do we make a perceptron learn to perform certain tasks? In this chapter, we are going behind the scenes of “Perceptron Learning Institute”. Keep scrolling!

In the last chapter, we learned that a perceptron can take in some inputs and spit out a valid output. How can we make it learn to perform tasks? To understand this, we will first discuss what the perceptron is abstractly.

On a high level, the perceptron can be seen as a mathematical function, taking in some inputs, some weights, a bias, and mixes them up mathematically to generate an output.

Since we have no control over the inputs (we cannot change the data we are learning from), we will try to change weights and bias so that the perceptron function will give out a good output for all the input we stuff into it.

The core of “teaching” a perceptron is to let it understand how far its actions or predictions are from the ground truth (desired results). To achieve this, we use a loss function to measure the performance of our perceptron. A commonly used loss function is called cross entropy, which is very good at tasks like classifying images.

Here, the cross entropy loss is visualized as the difference between the input (rectangle) and the output (triangle). To train the perceptron can be considered as the process of decreasing this difference. Once the loss is decreased to its minimum, the perceptron completes its learning.

There are many other loss functions, but all loss functions share a common behavior: the further off the prediction is from the ground truth, the bigger the output of the loss function is. In this way, we can try to minimize the loss by changing the weights and biases, and our perceptron will gradually improve until it reaches its capacity.

To minimize loss, we use an optimizer called Gradient Descent. The "hill" here depicts the relationship between the weights and bias of a perceptron and the loss. The higher upon the hill we are, the greater the loss is. Gradient Descent is a technique that directs us walking “downhill”, with the optimizer finding the steepest way downwards.

First, the Gradient Descent optimizer would look at the "hill" with respect to the data, and generate gradients, providing a direction downhill. Then, the optimizer picks a learning rate to decide how far to walk before stopping and redetermining the gradients. After many repetitions, we will slip our way down to reach a minimum loss.

Glossary

Loss Function: A function that determines the loss, a value representing how far the output predictions are from the ground truth.

Gradient: A group of numbers indicating which direction would be the “steepest” way to minimize loss (downhill).

Gradient Descent: An optimizer that uses gradients to adjust the weights and bias to minimize loss.
